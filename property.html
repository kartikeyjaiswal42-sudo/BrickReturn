Year-by-year simulation. Pick your method.

Investor: locked at fixed 8–8.5% CAGR (slider) — paid lump sum at end, like a bank FD. Buyer: grows at MIN(actual yield, 10%) — earns 9% if portfolio earns 9%, capped at 10% if portfolio earns more. How each method works

Three perspectives. Two methods. One investor outcome.

The two methods differ only in how the buyer's notional balance is tracked. The investor's lump-sum payout is identical in both. Pick the method that matches how you want to position the deal.

🏢
FOR YOU

BrickReturn™

Method 1 · Buyer-favoured

You end with — from a starting arbitrage of — (—× growth). Lower margin per unit, but the bigger rebate makes the deal easier to close. Use when scaling matters more than per-unit profit.

Method 2 · Balanced

You end with — (—× growth) — more value stays in the corpus because the buyer is paid less over the same tenure. Use in mature markets with organic demand and less need for aggressive incentives.

📈
FOR THEM

The Investor

Method 1

Receives — at maturity from a — commitment — a contractual 8.5% CAGR. Lump-sum paid at end of 12 years.

Method 2

Identical outcome — same —. The investor's payout is fully insulated from how you account for the buyer's rebate. Your method choice doesn't affect them.

✓ Both methods deliver the investor the same return.

The Homebuyer

Method 1

Receives — total over the tenure. Monthly rebate grows from — in Y1 to — by Y12 — —% off EMI on average. Bigger upside; great for wealth preservation.

Method 2

Receives — total. Monthly rebate grows more steadily, ending at — by Y12 — —% relief on average. Smaller but more predictable curve.

🎯
How to decide which method to offer

If you're entering a new market or attracting first-time customers, Method 1's bigger rebate is a stronger pitch. If you have steady buyer flow and want to maximise per-unit retention, Method 2 keeps more in the corpus. The investor sees no difference either way — the choice is entirely between you and the homebuyer.
